 Coherence is about how systems hold together

Most design systems focus on what interfaces look like.

Coherence focuses on how systems behave.

Because the real problem is not visual inconsistency.  
It is structural drift.

---

## The problem

Modern products are not single systems.

They are made up of:

- Interfaces
- Services
- Data pipelines
- Workflows
- Teams
- Regulations

Each layer evolves independently.

Over time, they stop aligning.

---

## What drift looks like

- Interfaces that don’t reflect system reality  
- Data that exists but cannot be used  
- Accessibility applied inconsistently  
- Design decisions that don’t scale across products  
- Teams solving the same problem in different ways  

Nothing is technically broken.

But the system no longer holds together.

---

## What Coherence does

Coherence introduces structure across layers.

It connects:

- Principles → how decisions are made  
- Tokens → how meaning is encoded  
- Components → how interfaces are built  
- Patterns → how products behave  
- Governance → how systems evolve  

This creates alignment, not just consistency.

---

## The shift

Most systems ask:

> How do we standardise UI?

Coherence asks:

> How do we maintain alignment across a system over time?

---

## Inclusion is not a feature

Accessibility is often treated as a checklist.

Coherence treats it as a system property.

That means:

- Tokens support contrast and readability  
- Components support keyboard and assistive tech  
- Patterns support real-world usage and edge cases  
- Content is clear and usable  

Inclusion is not added later.

It is built into the structure.

---

## Semantic over visual

A system built on visual naming does not scale.

“Green”, “Primary”, “Dark mode” — these are surface-level decisions.

Coherence uses semantic meaning:

- `surface.primary`
- `text.muted`
- `action.critical`
- `status.success`

This allows the system to evolve without breaking.

---

## Systems don’t fail all at once

They degrade slowly.

Small inconsistencies accumulate:

- a slightly different component
- a missing state
- an inaccessible interaction
- a duplicated pattern

Over time, this becomes systemic.

Coherence exists to prevent that.

---

## The outcome

When systems are coherent:

- Teams move faster without diverging  
- Products feel consistent without being rigid  
- Accessibility is reliable, not incidental  
- Design decisions scale across contexts
